## The Core Problem

**700 years of history shows the same pattern:**
Power distributes → Money concentrates → Money captures power → System serves money.

**Every constitutional system eventually gets captured financially.**

Why? Because they treat economic governance as separate from constitutional governance.

---

## Reposte's Solution: Constitutional Economics

**Economic structure is constitutional constraint, not business strategy.**

The way money flows is written into the constitution and cannot be changed to serve profit over principles.

## Dual Currency Model

### Two Different Tokens, Two Different Purposes

**£ (British Pound - Fiat Currency):**
- Used for practical operations
- Pays for infrastructure, development, services
- Traditional business currency

**RPT (Reposte Token - Governance Token):**
- Earned through contribution, cannot be purchased directly
- Represents voting power on constitutional amendments
- Revenue share from ecosystem value
- Governance rights proportional to contribution

### Why Two Currencies?

**Problem with fiat alone:** Whoever controls fiat currency (banks, investors) can capture the system through economic pressure.

**Problem with crypto alone:** Becomes pure speculation, disconnected from practical operation.

**Solution:** Fiat for operations, crypto for governance. Economic controllers cannot capture governance because governance token is earned, not purchased.

## Revenue Distribution (Constitutional Requirement)

**Minimum required distribution:**
- **70-80%** flows to user value creation
- **15-25%** for operational sustainability
- **5%** constitutional reserve for enforcement

**This is inverted from typical startups:**
- Traditional: ~70% to investors/operators, ~30% to value creation
- Reposte: ~70% to value creation, ~30% to operations

**Why:** System must serve users' truth-seeking, not extract value from users for investors.

**Constitutional protection:** This distribution cannot be changed to favor profit over users, even in economic crisis.

## Anti-Capture Constraints

### Hard Caps on Ownership:

**No investor can own >10% of governance rights**
- Prevents single investor capture
- Even with massive funding, cannot control

**Founding team combined cannot exceed 30% after Phase 2**
- Prevents founder capture
- Forces progressive decentralization

**Economic concentration triggers automatic dilution**
- If ownership approaches caps, new RPT issued to community
- Keeps power distributed

### What This Means:

Even if someone has billions to invest, they cannot:
- Buy governance control
- Override constitutional principles
- Capture the system for profit
- Force economic decisions

**Money cannot override constitution.**

## What Reposte CANNOT Do Economically

**Even in crisis, even with unanimous vote:**

### Cannot:
- Sell user data without consent
- Implement engagement optimization for revenue
- Accept investment requiring governance control
- Sacrifice constitutional principles for profit
- Override user sovereignty for monetization
- Change revenue distribution to favor investors
- Create paid tiers that violate constitutional principles

### Can:
- Reduce features temporarily to lower costs
- Request community funding with transparent accounting
- Open-source components to reduce burden
- Graceful shutdown with full data export (rather than violate principles)

**The principle:** System dies rather than compromises constitution for money.

   - Development project approval

## Economic Decision Governance

**Major economic decisions require community approval:**
- Changes to revenue distribution ratios
- New economic features or tokens
- Partnership agreements affecting constitutional compliance
- Major fundraising that could create capture risk

**Approval requirements:**
- 60% of governance token holders must approve
- Minimum 30% participation threshold
- 14-day comment period for community review
- Constitutional compliance analysis must be published

**All votes on-chain, publicly verifiable.**

## How This Prevents Historical Capture Pattern

**Historical pattern:**
1. System starts with good intentions
2. Needs funding to scale
3. Accepts investor money
4. Investors demand returns
5. System optimizes for profit
6. Constitutional principles erode
7. System captured

**Reposte prevention:**

1. Economic structure is constitutional (step 1)
2. Funding capped and aligned (step 2-3)
3. Investors cannot demand constitutional override (step 4)
4. System optimizes for truth-seeking (step 5)
5. Principles enforced by smart contracts (step 6)
6. Community can fork if capture attempted (step 7)

**The difference:** Constitution constrains economics, economics doesn't drive constitution.

## Common Questions

**Q: How can this be profitable if 70-80% goes to users?**  
A: Because sustainable value creation creates larger total value. Better to have 20% of a large sustainable pie than 70% of a small dying pie.

**Q: Won't investors avoid these constraints?**  
A: Mission-aligned investors will. Extractive investors should avoid—they're incompatible with the model. This is a feature, not a bug.

**Q: What if Reposte needs emergency funding?**  
A: Community funding, temporary feature reduction, or graceful shutdown. Never constitutional compromise for survival.

**Q: Can someone buy up all RPT on the market?**  
A: Anti-capture constraints trigger automatic dilution if concentration approaches caps. Also, RPT holders who earned it through contribution unlikely to sell to potential capturer.

**Q: How do you compete with free AI that violates all these principles?**  
A: By serving users who value sovereignty over convenience. Not everyone is the target market. Those who want constitutional protection will pay for it.
